What this is

An example set of the high-level topics we'd cover. This is a starting point for conversation, not a finished syllabus. Two arcs run in parallel, the doctrine and the hands-on skills, themed to each other week by week, with a semester-long capstone that ties them together.

The skills side follows one design principle. Teach each capability concretely on a single tool so it's genuinely learnable, then name the transferable skill every time. What students learn should survive whichever platform they use in practice.

I

The Doctrine Arc

Nick’s half

Not a survey of everything. A working understanding of how AI is governed, and where that lands in the areas you’ll practice. The two existing McKinney AI electives teach the law about AI; this half teaches the doctrine a practitioner actually needs.

Foundations

What AI is
A working technical mental model for lawyers: enough of how the systems work to reason about them in practice.

How AI is governed

The US regulatory reality
There’s no single AI statute. Governance comes from a patchwork of state laws and from agencies acting under existing authority, with export controls on advanced compute and model weights as one federal lever among them.
The EU AI Act & international comparators
The contrast case: what comprehensive, risk-tiered regulation looks like, and what it asks of the businesses you’ll advise.
Data governance & privacy
GDPR, the US state laws, biometrics. The deepest well in the course, because data governance touches nearly every practice area you might enter.

AI where you practice

Sector-specific regulation
Healthcare, financial services, employment: where AI use meets regimes a lawyer already has to know.
IP & copyright
Ownership, training-data provenance, and infringement exposure.
Contracting around AI
Vendor agreements, AI clauses, indemnities, and the allocation of risk when the tool is someone else’s.

When AI goes wrong

Liability & responsibility
Who is on the hook when AI gets it wrong, including misuse and bad-actor scenarios.
Litigation & evidence
Deepfakes, AI-generated filings, and the growing line of sanctions.
Touch points
Algorithmic bias & discrimination, and criminal-justice uses, surfaced as facets of the liability conversation rather than standalone units.

Professional responsibility

Professional responsibility
ABA Formal Opinion 512 and the duties it names, plus the fast-moving layer beneath it: state-bar guidance and court standing orders. Taught as a model for how a graduate stays current in their own jurisdiction.
II

The Skills Arc

Noah’s half

Claude-first and hands-on. We start by demystifying the model — it’s not magic, and you can see everything it’s doing — then learn Claude’s core functions one at a time, get fluent in Claude Code from the terminal up, and finish by building real legal workflows. Verification and confidentiality aren’t a separate unit; they’re built into every demo and every workflow. We teach on Claude because it’s the tool you’ll actually use, and the underlying skills transfer to whatever you adopt next.

Block 1

Demystify

It’s not magic — you can see everything
What it’s really doing
Next-token prediction, with no internal database of facts. We open the hood so the model stops feeling like magic and starts behaving like a tool you can predict and direct.
Failure modes, induced on purpose
Hallucination, sycophancy, and confident wrongness, triggered live in class so you recognize them on sight — and the reflex that answers them: never cite what you haven’t read, the verification turn the lawyers in Mata v. Avianca skipped.
Prompting, briefly
The difference a leading vs. a neutral prompt makes, shown by rebuilding a weak prompt into a reliable one. Enough craft to get honest output before we add any tooling.
Block 2

The core functions of Claude

Each one: an analogy, a demo, pushed to the max
Chat, done well
A sharp associate with no memory of yesterday. Turning a vague ask into a structured, sourced answer — the baseline most people never get past.
Projects
A matter file the assistant always has open. Stand one up, decide first what client information may touch the tool, load real documents, and watch recall improve across sessions.
Skills
A firm’s template or checklist, made runnable. Turn a task you repeat into a one-command skill.
Artifacts
A document that talks back. Draft and iterate real work product — a memo, a clause library, a timeline — in a live, editable space.
Research mode
A research assistant who shows their trail. Run a multi-step, sourced investigation, then check every source the way you’d check a junior’s.
Block 3

Claude Code, from zero

The terminal up — your core professional tool
The terminal, demystified
Core moves only, no prior command line assumed: move into a folder, step up or down a level, list what’s there, make a new folder, and open Claude inside it.
Install & first run
Getting Claude Code onto your own laptop and running it in a real folder — the setup that makes everything after it possible.
Slash commands, agents & cron jobs
What a slash command does, what an agent is, and what a cron job automates — the parts that turn Claude from a chat box into a system that does work for you.
How it differs from regular Claude
File-aware and agentic: it reads and writes your actual documents and runs multi-step tasks, where chat only talks. The capability worth real fluency for the rest of your career.
Cowork — the gentler on-ramp
Essentially Claude Code with a friendlier surface and less power, and no terminal required. Where less technical users will gravitate — worth knowing even as you build fluency with the full tool.
Block 4

Workflows — where the rubber meets the road

Doctrine, applied to real work

A few worked examples drawn from real legal work, where the doctrine from Nick’s half becomes a concrete build: the rule names a duty, so the workflow is designed to meet it. Verification gates and human checkpoints are part of every one.

Drafting under a governance rule
Generate, critique, constrain, verify — and catch the sector-blind draft (the contract that omits a clause a regulation requires). The doctrine names the duty; the workflow builds the check.
Document review & diligence at scale
Extraction with quote-and-cite discipline, catching the silent failures that sink review (“agrees to assign” becomes “owns”), with a supervision log that satisfies the duty to supervise.
Litigation prep, verified
Discovery triage with privilege flags and a deposition outline audited for what’s grounded vs. assumed — red-teaming your own brief so a fabricated cite never reaches the court.
Designing a defensible workflow
Decompose a task into green / yellow / red zones, decide where AI doesn’t go, and build the verification gates and human sign-off — the workflow you defend in the capstone.
