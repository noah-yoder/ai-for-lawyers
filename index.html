What this is

An example set of the high-level topics we'd cover—a starting point for our conversation, not a syllabus. Two interleaved arcs, the doctrine and the hands-on skills, themed to each other week by week, with a semester-long capstone that ties them together. Everything below is meant to be argued with.

The design principle on the skills side: teach each capability concretely on one tool so it's actually learnable, but name the transferable skill every time—so what students learn survives whichever platform they end up using in practice.

I

The Doctrine Arc

Nick’s half

Less a survey of everything, more a working understanding of how AI is actually governed and where that lands in the areas a graduate will practice. The two existing McKinney AI electives teach the law about AI; this half teaches only the doctrine a practitioner needs to make defensible decisions.

Foundations

What AI actually is
A working technical mental model for lawyers—approachable from either the technical or the practice-impact direction, by either instructor.

How AI is actually governed

The US regulatory reality
Why there’s no single AI statute—and how the government actually regulates: export controls on advanced compute and model weights as a primary federal lever, alongside the state-law patchwork and agency action.
The EU AI Act & international comparators
The contrast case—what comprehensive, risk-tiered regulation looks like, and what it asks of the businesses our students will advise.
Data governance & privacy
GDPR, the US state laws, biometrics. The deepest well in the course, because data governance touches every practice area an attorney might enter.

AI where you practice

Sector-specific regulation
Healthcare, financial services, employment—where AI use meets regimes a lawyer already has to know.
IP & copyright
Ownership, training-data provenance, and infringement exposure.
Contracting around AI
Vendor agreements, AI clauses, indemnities, and the allocation of risk when the tool is someone else’s.

When AI goes wrong

Liability & responsibility
Who is on the hook when AI gets it wrong—including the bad-actor and misuse dimension, the less-than-honest and less-than-careful user.
Litigation & evidence
Deepfakes, AI-generated filings, and the growing line of sanctions.
Touch points
Algorithmic bias & discrimination, and criminal-justice uses—surfaced as facets of the liability conversation rather than standalone units. This is an AI course, not a civil-rights course.

The ethics spine — runs under everything

Professional responsibility
ABA Formal Opinion 512 and the duties it names, plus the fast-moving layer beneath it—state-bar guidance and court standing orders—taught as the model for how a graduate stays current in their own jurisdiction.
II

The Skills Arc

Noah’s half

Organized by capability, not by use case—students learn to operate the instrument deeply, and the legal tasks become the vehicle. Free-first and tool-agnostic: every skill runs on the free tiers of ChatGPT, Claude, and NotebookLM, and each capability is named as a transferable skill so it survives whichever platform a student ends up using. Professional and paid features are demonstrated where they meaningfully differ—never required to complete the course.

Block 1

The basics

Roughly the first month — learn the instrument
Mental model & failure modes
How the model actually works—next-token prediction, no internal database of facts—and the three failures it produces: hallucination, sycophancy, and confident wrongness, induced on purpose in the lab so students recognize them on sight.
Prompting as a craft
Beyond “be specific.” The five-part calibrated prompt—role, task, facts, constraints, output format—and the difference a leading vs. neutral prompt makes, practiced by rebuilding a weak prompt into a reliable one.
Verification you can actually perform
Never cite what you haven’t read. A three-pass protocol—does the source exist, does it support the claim, is it still good law—run by hand against a planted memo, including the dangerous “real source, wrong proposition” miscite.
Data & confidentiality
Where your inputs actually go; tiering tools by what client information may touch them; sanitizing a document, then auditing whether it could still be re-identified.
Block 2

Doing the work

Real legal tasks, start to finish
Drafting with AI
The iteration loop—generate, critique, constrain, verify—and catching the sector-blind draft (the contract that quietly omits the clause a regulation requires), then translating the result into plain English a client can act on.
Document review & diligence
Extraction across a document set with quote-and-cite discipline, and the four silent failure modes that sink review—filled-in blanks, missed cross-references, stale extraction, and meaning-flattening paraphrase (“agrees to assign” becomes “owns”)—caught with a supervision log.
Litigation prep
AI discovery triage with privilege flags, auditing a deposition outline for what’s grounded in the record vs. assumed, and red-teaming your own brief—the verification turn the lawyers in Mata v. Avianca skipped.
Block 3

Designing & supervising workflows

From one-off prompts to a defensible process
Decomposition — and when not to use AI
Breaking a real task into green / yellow / red zones, with the red-zone triggers that keep a step in human hands, captured on a reusable workflow-design card. Knowing where AI doesn’t go is the skill that separates this from a tool tutorial.
Supervised review & vendor diligence
A five-lens protocol for critiquing an AI tool or its output, comparing a human reviewer against an AI one, and treating structured supervisory review as its own learnable skill.
QA rubrics
Building a six-to-eight-point quality checklist across the error families that actually recur—fabrication, mischaracterization, math and logic, internal inconsistency, omission, overclaiming, bias—then grading the AI’s own grading.
Demonstrated

Frontier capabilities

Shown in class — never required to pass

Everything above runs on free tiers. These higher-leverage capabilities are demonstrated so students know they exist and what they unlock—and anyone carrying Claude Pro can take them further—but a free-tier path is always provided.

Projects & persistent context
A curated knowledge base the assistant always has open. Like a matter file that never closes.
Skills & Artifacts
Reusable, packaged instructions—a firm’s templates and checklists made runnable—and a live, editable space for building real work product.
Grounded research
Working over real files with retrieval and sources—plus an honest look at why even grounded tools still mischaracterize a holding.
Claude Code
The agentic, file-aware frontier—a glimpse of where the most leverage will come from next.
Then it all comes together — the capstone ↓
III

The Capstone

Block 4 · the spine

Everything before this taught a capability in isolation. The capstone is the only place students learn orchestration—composing those capabilities into a workflow that does real legal work end to end, then defending it like a practitioner.

A defended workflow

Build it, break it, defend it.

Each student designs an AI-assisted legal workflow for a real scenario, composes it from the skills above—task decomposition into green/yellow/red zones, verification gates, and human checkpoints, reaching for advanced capabilities like Projects or Claude Code where they’re available and actually help—then stress-tests it against adversarial cases and defends every design choice.

Scope→ Compose→ Orchestrate→ Stress-test→ Defend→ Reflect

The deliverable: a working workflow, a memo defending the design, and a live oral defense with Q&A. The student who finds where their own workflow breaks scores higher than the one whose demo went smoothly—a test that fails is a good result.

Because one workflow touches every duty, the capstone is where the whole ethics spine converges:
Rule 1.1Competence — why this workflow, why these tools
Rule 1.6Confidentiality — what data touched what, and how
Rule 1.4Communication — what the client is told about the AI use
Rule 5.1/5.3Supervision — the checkpoints and human sign-off
Rule 3.3/3.1Candor & meritorious claims — the verification gates that keep a fabricated cite out of a filing
Rule 1.5Fees — how AI efficiency shows up in a bill, honestly
IV

For Our Conversation

where I want your read

The shape above is mine to argue, not to impose. A handful of genuine forks where your judgment—and the faculty’s—should drive:

The doctrine shape

Does the “touch points” framing land—liability as the through-line, with bias and criminal justice as facets rather than their own weeks? And does leading the US story with export controls match how you’d teach the regulatory landscape?

Who teaches what

Your wheelhouse covers regulation, privacy, contracting, and litigation cleanly. IP/copyright, liability/tort, and the bias touch points need a plan—you stretch, a guest lecturer, or a faculty co-instructor. Worth naming owners early.

Student tooling

The skills half is built free-first—every lab runs on the free tiers of ChatGPT, Claude, and NotebookLM, so cost is never a barrier to completing the course. Claude Pro (~$20/mo) and an enterprise tier (a real document-management system, connectors) unlock the frontier capabilities we demo—the open question is how much of that the school funds versus leaves as optional enrichment.

The capstone as the graded spine

A built workflow plus a defense memo plus a live oral defense. Does that satisfy the school’s expectations for a 3-credit graded deliverable, or do we pair it with something more traditional?

Positioning & the pilot

Confirming this lands as a third, differentiated applied elective alongside the two doctrinal ones—the bifurcated weekly format, and a Fall 2026 pilot before a permanent catalog listing.
