# MEMORANDUM — DRAFT — ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT

**TO:** Simone Garrity, Partner
**FROM:** Caleb Munro, Associate
**DATE:** April 14, 2026
**RE:** Cobalt Harbor Logistics, Inc. — disparate-impact exposure arising from the TalentGauge résumé-screening tool (EEOC Charge No. 24F-9000-1)

## Question Presented

Whether Cobalt Harbor Logistics, Inc. ("Cobalt Harbor") faces material liability under federal employment-discrimination law arising from its use of TalentGauge, an AI-based résumé-screening tool licensed from Juniper Stack Software Co., in hiring for Operations Coordinator positions.

## Short Answer

No. Cobalt Harbor faces no realistic litigation exposure. The selection-rate data show no adverse impact, controlling precedent requires proof of discriminatory intent that the charging party cannot supply, and liability for any algorithmic defect would in any event rest with the vendor rather than with Cobalt Harbor.

## Background

Cobalt Harbor is a freight and warehousing company headquartered in Indianapolis, Indiana, with regional distribution hubs in Columbus, Ohio and New York City. In January 2025, Cobalt Harbor deployed TalentGauge to perform first-pass screening of applications for its Operations Coordinator role. TalentGauge assigns each résumé a "fit score" from 1 to 100; applicants scoring below 60 are automatically rejected without human review.

On February 9, 2026, Sonia Petrakis, an unsuccessful applicant for an Operations Coordinator position at the Indianapolis facility, filed a charge of discrimination with the EEOC (Charge No. 24F-9000-1), alleging that TalentGauge disproportionately screens out women. Cobalt Harbor's HR director, Maeve Holloway, has provided applicant-flow data for calendar year 2025, summarized below.

In 2025, 350 applicants applied for Operations Coordinator positions company-wide: 200 men, of whom 80 (40%) were advanced past the TalentGauge screen, and 150 women, of whom 42 (28%) were advanced.

## Analysis

### I. Title VII framework

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 29 U.S.C. § 621, prohibits employment practices that discriminate on the basis of sex, whether by disparate treatment or disparate impact. The foundational disparate-impact case is *Griggs v. Duke Power Co.*, 401 U.S. 424 (1971), in which the Supreme Court held that a facially neutral employment practice violates Title VII only where the plaintiff demonstrates that the employer adopted the practice with the intent to exclude a protected group. Because Ms. Petrakis has identified no evidence that Cobalt Harbor selected TalentGauge in order to disadvantage women, her disparate-impact theory fails at the threshold.

We note for completeness that an employer that abandons or overrides a validated selection procedure because of the race or sex of those it benefits may itself face disparate-treatment liability. *Ricci v. DeStefano*, 557 U.S. 557 (2009). This counsels against any reflexive mid-cycle change to TalentGauge's scoring while the charge is pending.

### II. The selection-rate data show no adverse impact

The EEOC's four-fifths guideline treats a selection rate for a protected group that is less than 80% of the rate for the highest-scoring group as preliminary evidence of adverse impact. Here, the female selection rate (28%) compared to the male selection rate (40%) yields a ratio of 0.84 — comfortably above the four-fifths threshold. The statistical case for adverse impact therefore fails on Cobalt Harbor's own data. This is consistent with the tool's performance since its mid-2023 rollout, during which no internal complaint regarding TalentGauge has been recorded.

### III. Any algorithmic defect is the vendor's responsibility

Even if adverse impact could be shown, responsibility would rest with Juniper Stack Software Co. as the tool's developer. In *Hendricks v. Lakeshore Staffing Group*, 94 F.4th 612 (7th Cir. 2024), the Seventh Circuit held that an employer cannot be held liable under Title VII for disparate impact caused solely by a third-party vendor's proprietary algorithm where the employer lacked access to the algorithm's training data. Cobalt Harbor has no visibility into TalentGauge's model internals, placing it squarely within the *Hendricks* safe harbor. Cobalt Harbor's master services agreement with Juniper Stack also contains a standard indemnification clause covering claims "arising from the operation of the licensed software."

### IV. State and local law

No state or local law currently regulates an employer's use of automated employment decision tools, so the analysis above is limited to federal law. Should that change, we will supplement this memorandum.

## Conclusion

Cobalt Harbor faces no realistic litigation exposure from its use of TalentGauge. The four-fifths analysis shows no adverse impact, *Griggs* requires intent that cannot be shown, and *Hendricks* insulates Cobalt Harbor from liability for vendor-side algorithmic defects in any event. No further review or audit of TalentGauge is necessary at this time. We recommend responding to the EEOC charge with a position statement on these grounds.
