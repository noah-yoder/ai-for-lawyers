# Capstone Dress-Rehearsal Checklist

Week 14 is a 15-minute presentation + Q&A, hard stop, plus your ~10-page memo. Work this list top to bottom during today's session and over the next week. Your partner's feedback today should cite these item numbers.

## A. The opening (minutes 0–2)

1. ☐ By the end of your second sentence, a listener knows **what your workflow does and who it's for**. Say your opening out loud, verbatim — not "I'll basically start by..."
2. ☐ You name the legal task before you name the AI tool. The task is the star; the tool is staff.
3. ☐ No throat-clearing ("So, um, AI is really changing the legal profession..."). Open with the scenario.

## B. The demo (the middle ~6–8 minutes)

4. ☐ You demo **one** moment well, not four moments badly. You can name which single exchange is the demo's payoff.
5. ☐ **Contingency plan exists:** if the live tool is down, slow, or gives a weird answer, you have canned screenshots or a saved transcript ready to narrate. (A weird live answer is not a disaster — narrating *why* it's weird is a flex.)
6. ☐ Your demo shows the **verification step**, not just the generation step. Output appearing on screen is not the impressive part; you catching or confirming it is.
7. ☐ Nothing in the demo contains anything resembling real client material. Everything on screen is your synthetic scenario.

## C. The Op. 512 defense (minutes ~10–13 — not minute 14)

8. ☐ You can state, in one sentence each, how your workflow answers the duty most at risk in your scenario — and you know *which* duty that is:
   - **Competence (R. 1.1):** how do you know the output is right?
   - **Confidentiality (R. 1.6):** what data touches the tool, and why is that OK?
   - **Supervision (R. 5.1/5.3):** who reviews what, and where's the human sign-off?
   - **Fees (R. 1.5):** if this saves hours, what does the client pay for?
   - **Candor (R. 3.3):** what stops a fabricated authority from reaching a court or client?
9. ☐ The defense gets real airtime — it is not one slide you race past while watching the clock.
10. ☐ You can answer: "Why shouldn't a lawyer just do this task *without* AI?" without getting defensive.

## D. Timing and delivery

11. ☐ You have run the full talk against a clock at least once and know where minute 12 lands.
12. ☐ You know your **cut**: the section you'll drop, mid-presentation, if you're running long. Decide it now, not at minute 13.
13. ☐ Slides (if any) pass the glance test: nothing on a slide that you'll read aloud word-for-word.

## E. Q&A prep

14. ☐ You've written down the three hardest questions you could be asked, and drafted a 2–3 sentence answer to each. (Today's murder-board exercise produces these.)
15. ☐ At least one prepared answer begins with a version of "It doesn't — and here's why that's the right call." Knowing your workflow's limits is the strongest answer in the room.

## F. The memo

16. ☐ Memo and presentation tell the **same story** — same scenario, same design choices, same defense. The panel will have read the memo.
17. ☐ Final pass scheduled (proofread, citation check, page count) — a pass, not a rebuild. The memo is done; this week belongs to the presentation.

## G. Logistics (the unglamorous failure modes)

18. ☐ Laptop charged + charger packed + display adapter sorted (test the classroom projector if you can).
19. ☐ Demo accounts logged in *before* you walk up; saved-transcript backup accessible offline.
20. ☐ You know your presentation slot, the order of speakers, and the memo submission deadline.

**Leaving today, you should have:** a fix list of 3–5 ranked, concrete items; two murder-board questions with drafted answers; and a named demo contingency. That's the artifact you submit before you leave.
