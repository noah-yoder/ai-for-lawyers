Defendant Meridian Gearworks LLC's Opposition to Plaintiff's
Motion for Partial Summary Judgment

I. Cobalt Harbor's warranty claim fails for lack of timely notice.

Summary judgment is appropriate only where no reasonable jury could
return a verdict for the nonmoving party. Anderson v. Liberty Lobby,
Inc., 477 U.S. 242, 248 (1986). Plaintiff cannot carry that burden,
because its express-warranty claim fails at the threshold: a buyer
who accepts goods "must within a reasonable time after he discovers
or should have discovered any breach notify the seller of breach or
be barred from any remedy." Ind. Code § 26-1-2-607(3)(a).

The undisputed record establishes that Cobalt Harbor first notified
Meridian of any claimed defect on October 13, 2025 — more than seven
months after delivery, and more than five months after the conveyor
problems Cobalt Harbor now alleges began. Before that letter, Cobalt
Harbor said nothing. Indiana courts hold that notice given more than
thirty days after discovery of an alleged defect is untimely as a
matter of law. Hollins v. Tri-State Conveyor Systems, Inc., 87 N.E.3d
1144, 1149 (Ind. Ct. App. 2017). Seven months is far beyond any
reasonable period.

Nor can Cobalt Harbor manufacture a fact dispute on the merits.
Meridian's quality-assurance records confirm that the Lot 88 drive
assemblies conformed to all contractual specifications at the time of
delivery. Plaintiff offers nothing but the say-so of its own
operations staff to suggest otherwise. Because timely notice is a
condition precedent to any remedy and none was given, partial summary
judgment must be denied.
